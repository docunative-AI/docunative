Finding 01 · Headline Result
The Language Match Discovery
Asking questions in the document's own language improves retrieval from ~39% to ~99%. This reveals the primary bottleneck in cross-lingual offline RAG.
Recall@3 — Did the retriever find the right paragraph?
Eval 1: English questions on native docs  ·  Eval 2: Native questions on native docs
Chinese (zh)
Hindi (hi)
Polish (pl)
Key Insight
Chinese Recall@3 jumped from 39.0% → 99.9% when questions are asked in Chinese. Hindi went from 44.4% → 94.3%. Polish from 65.7% → 94.9%. BGE-M3's cross-lingual embeddings are powerful but same-language retrieval is near-perfect. The future direction: translate the user's question into the document language before retrieval — entirely offline.
Finding 02 · Hypothesis 2
Validating Tiny Aya's Balancing
Despite a measurable internal training proportion gradient (1.9% → 1.7% → 1.4%), Hindi and Polish performance is nearly flat. This independently validates Cohere's data-balancing claim.
Token F1 by Language — Eval 1 vs Eval 2
Language Gradient Summary
Language Training % Eval 1 F1 Eval 2 F1
zh · Chinese 1.9% 0.346 0.532
hi · Hindi 1.7% 0.149 0.214
pl · Polish 1.4% 0.179 0.221
Verdict
Hindi and Polish F1 are nearly identical in both evals (0.149 vs 0.179, 0.214 vs 0.221) — within noise margin. The flat curve between hi and pl independently validates Cohere's claim: deliberate data-balancing stops low-resource languages from falling off a cliff.
Finding 03 · Most Memorable
The Cautious AI — Safety by Design
In Polish — the lowest-resource language — Tiny Aya refuses to answer 18% of the time in Eval 2. It found the right paragraph (Recall@3 = 94.9%) but chose not to answer. Caution, not hallucination.

Why This Matters for Legal Tech
Polish Recall@3 = 94.9% — the model found the right paragraph. But it refused to answer 18.1% of the time. Instead of hallucinating because it lacked Polish training data, Tiny Aya became more cautious. For a migrant reading an immigration letter, an AI that says nothing is safer than an AI that says something wrong.
Finding 04 · Hypothesis 1
Specialist vs Generalist on Hindi
Tiny Aya Fire (South Asian specialist, 5.8% Hindi training data) vs Global on Hindi legal documents. Regional specialisation improves precision, not overall accuracy.
Fire vs Global — Hindi metrics comparison
Head-to-Head · Hindi only · 1,200 pairs each
Metric Global Fire Winner
Token F1 0.147 0.136 Global ↑
Exact Match 9.6% 12.2% Fire ↑
Recall@3 44.4% 44.4% Tied
Refusal % 6.4% 4.4% Fire ↑
Entailment % 11.0% 8.9% Global ↑
Verdict
F1 scores are nearly tied. But Fire wins on Exact Match (+2.6%) and Refusal Rate (-2.0%). Regional specialisation makes the model more precise and more confident — even without improving overall token-level accuracy.

Notable
Immigration documents have the highest F1 in Eval 2 (0.360) but had near-zero Recall in Eval 1 for Hindi (0.020). This confirms the retrieval failure in Eval 1 was cross-lingual mismatch, not a fundamental limitation of BGE-M3. Health insurance documents have the highest entailment rate — the model's answers are most grounded in this domain.
